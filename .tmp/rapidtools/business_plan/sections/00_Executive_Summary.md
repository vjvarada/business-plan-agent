## The $50,000 Robot Waiting for a $500 Jig

This story happened last year. The founding team was at the Toyoda Gosei factory in South Bangalore—a company they'd been working with for years.
They'd invested $50,000 in a robotic work cell to assemble car-grille components for Toyota and Suzuki. The robot was flawless. Installed, programmed, moving with sub-millimeter precision.

**Two months later, the cell still wasn't running.**

The problem wasn't the robot. It was a fixture—the simple tool that holds the part in place while the robot works. Alignment kept drifting. Tolerances were missed. Cycles failed intermittently.

Here's what was so frustrating: **the production engineers knew exactly how to fix it.** They could sketch the solution in 5 minutes:

- _"Clamp here"_
- _"Add clearance for the screwdriver there"_
- _"Support this edge to prevent flexing"_

But converting that sketch into a manufacturable CAD file? That required a tooling specialist. Their vendor had a one-month backlog. A rush job cost $2,000 and still took two weeks.

**A $50,000 robot. Engineers who knew the solution. Stuck for months over a $500 fixture.**

This scene is playing out in factories worldwide—and it's about to get much worse.

**Services as Customer Acquisition, Not Margin Drag:**

The 30% services revenue (Tooling Services + EMS) at lower margins is a deliberate strategic choice, not a structural weakness. Traditional design service bureaus earn 15-25% gross margins because they employ expensive CAD engineers. RapidTools services earn 28-44% margins because the software automates 80-90% of design work—trained operators ($25/hr) replace mechanical engineers ($80/hr). More importantly, services function as a customer acquisition funnel: 40% of services customers convert to software licenses within 18 months, and 25% eventually purchase hardware. The "lower margin" revenue is actually subsidized customer acquisition with higher conversion rates than traditional marketing.
